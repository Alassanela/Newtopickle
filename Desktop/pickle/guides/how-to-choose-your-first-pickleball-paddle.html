How to choose your first pickleball paddle

What matters, and what does not

Paddle shopping is where beginners waste money. There are hundreds of options and a lot of loud marketing. Let me cut through it.

Do not overspend

You will see paddles at $250 and up, with talk of carbon fiber and spin numbers. Ignore all of it for now. You are new. You cannot use what those paddles offer yet, and you may not even like how you play until you have a few weeks in. A $40 to $80 paddle is plenty to start.

The three things that matter

  • Weight. Aim for a midweight paddle, roughly 7.8 to 8.3 ounces. Too heavy tires your arm. Too light gives you no power. Midweight is the safe middle.
  • Grip size. Small hands want a smaller grip. If the grip is too big, your hand cramps. If in doubt, go a touch smaller. You can add tape to build it up, but you cannot shave it down.
  • Shape. Pick a standard, wider shape, not the long skinny "pro" shape. A wider paddle gives you a bigger sweet spot, which means more forgiving hits while you learn.

What you can ignore

Spin ratings, exotic materials, and "pro tour" branding. None of that helps a beginner. A basic fiberglass or composite paddle is fine and will last you months.

My 3 beginner picks

These are the three paddles I would point a new player to right now. All under $100, all well reviewed by people who test paddles for a living, and none of them are the overpriced pro models.

Budget pick: Franklin Signature (around $50)

If you just want to try the sport without spending much, this is the safe cheap option. It is a fiberglass paddle with a comfortable grip and a decent sweet spot, and it is approved for tournament play. It will not spin the ball like the carbon paddles, but as a brand new player you will not notice that yet. Buy it, play for a month, and upgrade only if you fall in love with the game.

Best overall: Vatic Pro Prism Flash 16mm (around $90)

This paddle tops nearly every "best beginner paddle" list in 2026, and for good reason. It is a carbon fiber paddle with a 16mm core, which means soft, controlled hits that are easy to keep in the court. It is forgiving when you miss the center, and it is good enough that many intermediate players still use it. If you know you are going to stick with pickleball, start here and you will not need a new paddle for a year or more.

Most forgiving: 11SIX24 Pegasus Jelly Bean (around $100)

This one has a wide body shape, which gives it one of the biggest sweet spots you can buy at any price. That matters for beginners: a bigger sweet spot means fewer mishits and fewer embarrassing pop-ups. It is a control paddle, so it will not add power for you, but control is what wins beginner games anyway. If your main worry is "what if I keep missing the ball," this is your paddle.

Check price on Amazon

One honest note: paddle prices move around a lot, and stock comes and goes. If one of these is unavailable or the price jumped, any midweight carbon fiber paddle with a 16mm core in the $60 to $100 range will serve you well.

Best move of all: try before you buy

Go to open play and ask to hold a few people's paddles between games. Pickleball players love talking gear and will happily let you feel the weight. Ten minutes of that teaches you more than any review.

Buy one honest, midweight paddle. Play for a month. Only then think about an upgrade.
